Sharing your work and progress openly — even when it's messy or incomplete — builds community, creates accountability, and helps others who are on a similar path. You don't have to have it all figured out to share what you're doing.

## Why this matters

Most developers share their finished work. But a lot of the most useful content comes from people sharing the in-between: the confusing part they're working through, the approach they're trying, the small wins that wouldn't feel worth writing about on their own.

Building and learning in public does a few things:

- It gives you a record of your progress that you can look back on.
- It builds your presence in the community in a way that feels genuine rather than performative.
- It creates accountability — knowing you'll share your progress makes you more likely to make it.
- It helps other people who are in the same place you were, even if they never tell you.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## What to share

You don't need a polished product or a big announcement. Small, honest updates are often the most valuable:

- What you're working on this week
- A bug you just solved and how you found it
- Something you tried that didn't work
- A tool or technique you just discovered
- A question you're sitting with
- A small piece of progress you're proud of

The goal is consistency over time, not perfection in the moment.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Daily standup as a starting point

One of the best habits for building in public is a daily or weekly standup — a short update that answers a few simple questions:

- What did I work on yesterday (or this week)?
- What am I working on today (or this week)?
- Is there anything blocking me?

You don't need an audience for this to be useful. Writing it down for yourself creates clarity. Sharing it publicly creates connection.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Where to share

You have options, and none of them require a big platform:

- **Social media** — short updates on Twitter/X, LinkedIn, or Mastodon. Use hashtags like `#buildinpublic` or `#100DaysOfCode` to find others doing the same.
- **A personal blog** — a weekly or monthly "what I worked on" post is a great place to start.
- **[DEV.to](http://DEV.to) or Hashnode** — easy to publish and already have developer audiences.
- **GitHub** — keeping your commit history active and writing good commit messages is its own form of building in public.
- **Community spaces** — sharing in a Slack or Discord community where people know you is lower stakes and often more rewarding.

Start with wherever you're already spending time. You don't have to be everywhere.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Preparing a demo

Sharing what you've built in a live or recorded demo is a powerful way to show your work. A few things that make demos go smoothly:

**Before you start:**
- Test the exact workflow you plan to show
- Close anything that might distract — Slack, email, notifications
- Check your mic and camera if it's a video
- Make sure your text is large enough for someone on a small screen to read

**During the demo:**
- Explain what you're showing before you show it. Give context.
- It's okay to say "this is still in progress" or "I know this part isn't perfect yet."
- Slow down. Demos almost always benefit from a slower pace.

**Async demos** (a recording you share rather than presenting live) are a great option. [Loom](https://www.loom.com/) makes it easy to record your screen with a quick voiceover.

</TextContainer>

## Keep going

The hardest part of building in public is the first few posts, when it feels like no one is watching and you're not sure what you're doing. That feeling passes. Keep sharing, keep building, and give it time.

The record you create — of what you worked on, what you figured out, what you built — becomes one of the most valuable things you have. You'll look back on it and be glad you did it.
