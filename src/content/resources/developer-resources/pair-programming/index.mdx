<LeadText>

Pair programming is more than just coding with someone else. It's a practice of communication, teaching, learning, and growing together. Whether you're pairing with someone more experienced, less experienced, or at a similar level, you'll come away with more than you started with.

</LeadText>

<TextContainer>

## Why pair?

Pairing has real benefits that solo work doesn't:

- **Faster problem-solving.** Two perspectives catch things one misses. Bugs that would take hours solo often get found in minutes with a partner.
- **Knowledge transfer.** Pairing with someone more experienced is one of the fastest ways to learn. Pairing with someone less experienced cements your own understanding.
- **Better code.** When you have to explain your decisions out loud, you make better decisions.
- **Accountability and momentum.** It's harder to get stuck or distracted when someone is working with you.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## The driver and navigator model

The most common pairing structure:

- **Driver** — the person at the keyboard, writing the code
- **Navigator** — the person thinking ahead, reviewing, suggesting, and catching mistakes

The driver focuses on implementation. The navigator focuses on direction and quality. Roles should switch regularly — every 15–30 minutes works well.

This model works for in-person and remote pairing. The key is that both people stay engaged the entire time.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## What to pair on

You can pair on almost anything. Good starting points:

- An open-source issue you want to tackle but feel uncertain about
- A bug you're stuck on
- A LeetCode problem or code challenge
- A feature in a personal or side project
- A code review where you want to talk through the changes

Pairing doesn't require a big, complex problem. Short, focused sessions on a specific task work well, especially if you're new to pairing.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Making pairing work

**Before the session:**

- Agree on what you're working on and roughly how long
- Make sure your setup works — screen sharing, audio, editor access
- Set expectations: this is collaborative, not a performance review

**During the session:**

- Communicate. Say what you're thinking, even if it's half-formed.
- Ask questions freely. "Why are you doing it that way?" is a great question, not a challenge.
- Switch roles. Don't let one person drive the entire session.
- Take breaks. Pairing is mentally intense. A 5-minute break mid-session helps.

**After the session:**

- Do a quick retro. What worked? What would you change?
- Follow up on anything you learned or want to explore more.

</TextContainer>

## Remote pairing tools

Remote pairing works well with the right tools:

- **[VS Code Live Share](https://visualstudio.microsoft.com/services/live-share/)** — real-time collaborative editing in VS Code, free
- **[Tuple](https://tuple.app/)** — purpose-built remote pairing app for macOS and Linux, fast and low-latency
- **[GitHub Codespaces](https://github.com/features/codespaces)** — collaborative cloud development environment
- **[Zoom](https://zoom.us/) or [Google Meet](https://meet.google.com/)** — screen sharing works for quick sessions if you don't need full editor collaboration

VS Code Live Share is the easiest free option for most cases.

</TextContainer>
