## The purpose of code review

Code review isn't primarily about finding bugs (though that's a benefit). It's about:

- Sharing knowledge across the team
- Maintaining a consistent codebase
- Catching issues before they reach production
- Creating a record of why decisions were made
- Helping everyone on the team level up

When done well, code review is a conversation — not a gatekeeping mechanism.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Giving good feedback

The reviewer's job is to be helpful, not to be right. A few principles:

**Be specific.** "This is confusing" isn't actionable. "This variable name doesn't describe what it holds — could you rename it to something that reflects its purpose?" is.

**Explain the why.** Don't just say what to change. Explain why it matters. The author learns more, and you might be wrong.

**Distinguish between required and optional.** Make it clear when something is a blocker versus a suggestion. "Required: this will cause a null pointer exception" vs "Optional: this could be simplified with Array.map."

**Ask questions.** If something seems odd, ask about it before assuming it's wrong. "I haven't seen this pattern before — can you walk me through the reasoning?" opens a conversation.

**Acknowledge good work.** If something is well-done, say so. Positive feedback is useful and not given often enough.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Receiving feedback

Being reviewed can feel personal. It isn't. A few things that help:

- **Separate yourself from your code.** A critique of a function is not a critique of you.
- **Ask for clarification when you need it.** If feedback isn't clear, ask. Don't guess.
- **Say thank you.** Even when feedback is hard to receive, it usually comes from someone trying to help.
- **You don't have to agree.** It's okay to push back on feedback respectfully. Explain your reasoning.
- **Don't take it personally when your PR has lots of comments.** A thorough review is a good thing.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## What to look for in a review

A helpful checklist when reviewing code:

- **Does it work?** Does it do what it's supposed to do?
- **Is it readable?** Can someone unfamiliar with this area understand what it's doing?
- **Are there edge cases?** What happens with empty input, large data, or unexpected values?
- **Is it consistent?** Does it follow the patterns used elsewhere in the codebase?
- **Are there tests?** Are the tests meaningful, or do they just cover the happy path?
- **Is there documentation?** For public APIs or complex logic, is there enough context?

You don't need to catch everything. Focus on the things that matter most.

</TextContainer>

## Code review as a learning tool

One of the fastest ways to grow as a developer is to review code you didn't write. You'll encounter patterns, approaches, and solutions you wouldn't have thought of on your own.

Volunteer to review PRs in open source projects. Ask to be added as a reviewer at work. Read the code review comments on others' PRs, even when you're not involved. It all counts.
