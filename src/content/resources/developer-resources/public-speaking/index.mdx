## Why it's worth doing

Public speaking feels high-stakes, which is exactly why it's valuable. When you explain something out loud to an audience, you discover what you actually understand versus what you only think you understand.

Beyond that, speaking:

- Builds visibility in your field without requiring a large social media presence
- Creates connections with people in your community and industry
- Gives you a concrete reason to go deep on a topic
- Builds the kind of confidence that carries over into interviews, meetings, and code reviews

You don't have to become a conference keynote speaker. A five-minute lightning talk to 20 people at a meetup counts.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Start small

The best way to get comfortable speaking is to start at a scale where the stakes feel low:

- **Lightning talks** — 5 minutes, no pressure. A great first format. Virtual Coffee hosts these regularly.
- **Lunch & Learns** — present to a small group of people you already know.
- **Meetups and community calls** — local or online, these are welcoming and low-stakes.
- **Recorded presentations** — recording a short video walkthrough lets you practice without a live audience.

Each of these gives you experience and builds toward larger opportunities if you want them.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Choosing a topic

You don't need a revolutionary idea. You need something you know more about than your audience does, or a perspective that's genuinely yours.

Good starting points:

- Something you just learned and had to figure out the hard way
- A project you built and what you learned from it
- A tool or technique you use that others might not know about
- A concept you had to explain to a colleague recently

The best talks are specific. "How I debugged a memory leak in a Node.js app" is a better talk than "Memory management in JavaScript."

</TextContainer>

## Submitting to a CFP

A CFP (Call for Papers or Call for Proposals) is how most conferences select speakers. The process can feel intimidating, but it's more accessible than it looks.

**What to include in a proposal:**

- A clear, specific title
- A short abstract (2–4 sentences) describing what you'll cover and why it matters
- Who the audience is and what they'll take away
- Your relevant background — you don't need to be famous, just credible on the topic

**A few tips:**

- Submit to multiple CFPs. Rejection is normal and common for everyone.
- Look for CFPs that specifically welcome first-time speakers — many do.
- Ask for feedback if your proposal is rejected. Some conferences provide it.

Virtual Coffee's [Lightning Talk Guide](/assets/pdfs/lightning-talk-guide.pdf) is a good resource for structuring a talk proposal.

</TextContainer>

## Preparing your talk

Once you have a speaking slot:

- **Know your opening cold.** The first 60 seconds set the tone. Practice it until it's automatic.
- **Structure clearly.** What are you covering, and in what order? Tell people what you're going to say, say it, then remind them what you said.
- **Slide design.** Less text is better. Each slide should have one idea. Large fonts.
- **Practice out loud.** Reading slides in your head is not practice. Speaking to a wall or a friend is.
- **Time yourself.** It's easy to go over. Know where you can cut if needed.

It's okay to use notes. It's okay to make mistakes. Audiences are more forgiving than speakers expect.

</TextContainer>

## You know more than you think

The most common reason developers don't speak is that they don't think they have anything worth saying. That's almost never true. If you've learned something, struggled with something, or built something — that's a talk.

Start with a lightning talk. Go from there. The Virtual Coffee community is a great place to practice with a supportive audience.
