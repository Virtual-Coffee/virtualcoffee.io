## Why blog as a developer?

Blogging creates a compounding return. Each post you write:

- Helps you understand the topic more deeply by forcing you to articulate it
- Creates a record of your growth that you can look back on
- Helps someone else who is searching for the same thing you just figured out
- Builds a body of work that shows how you think and what you know

You don't need a large audience for blogging to be valuable. Even a small readership — or just your future self — is worth writing for.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## What to write about

The most common blogging block is not knowing what to write about. Here are approaches that work:

- **Document what you just learned.** If you spent time figuring something out, write it up. That version of you who was confused is your audience.
- **Write a tutorial you wish existed.** If you found the existing docs confusing, write the explanation you needed.
- **Share your process.** How you approached a problem, what you tried, and what worked — this is valuable even when the problem is simple.
- **Reflect on a project.** What did you build, what did you learn, what would you do differently?
- **Summarize something you read.** Synthesizing and explaining a concept in your own words is useful for readers and for you.

The best blog topics are usually just one level above where you are right now. You don't need to be a senior engineer to write something worth reading.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Where to publish

You have good options depending on how much you want to own your platform:

- **[DEV.to](https://dev.to/)** — free, developer-focused, easy to get started, built-in community
- **[Hashnode](https://hashnode.com/)** — similar to DEV, with the option to use a custom domain
- **[Medium](https://medium.com/)** — larger general audience, some posts require a paywall
- **A personal site** — more ownership and control, more setup required; good options include Astro, Jekyll, or Hugo with GitHub Pages

DEV.to is often the easiest starting point. You can always migrate later.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Getting your first post done

The hardest post is the first one. A few things that help:

- **Start with a small topic.** One function, one concept, one bug you fixed. The post doesn't have to cover everything.
- **Write like you're explaining to a friend.** Conversational is fine. Perfect prose is not required.
- **Don't wait until it's perfect.** Publish something good enough and improve over time.
- **Use a simple structure.** What is it? Why does it matter? How does it work? That's enough.

A post that helps one person is better than a perfect post that never gets published.

</TextContainer>

<TextContainer>

## Building a habit

Publishing occasionally is fine, but consistency over time is where blogging really pays off. Some approaches:

- Set a target — one post per month is achievable for most people
- Keep a running list of ideas so you're never starting from zero
- Write about what you're already doing, not as an extra task
- Share in community — posting in a Slack or Discord after you publish keeps you accountable

</TextContainer>
